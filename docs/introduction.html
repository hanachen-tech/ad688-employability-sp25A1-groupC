Research Introduction: Salary & Compensation Trends

Author

Afreen Alam, Lo Ying Wu, Tsyr Rau Chen

Published

May 31, 2026

Why is this topic important?

In recent years, persistent inflation and rising cost-of-living pressures have placed compensation at the center of public and academic debate. As everyday expenses such as housing and groceries continue to climb, workers across industries have increasingly demanded higher wages to maintain their purchasing power. The postpandemic surge in wage growth is primarily driven by inflation pass-through (DeLuca and Van Zandweghe (2023)), meaning rising prices directly translate into upward pressure on salaries. This ongoing tension between inflation and real wages makes compensation trends not just an economic concern, but a lived reality for millions of workers. It is precisely this urgency that motivated us to examine salary and compensation as a research topic. Understanding how and why wages change in response to broader economic pressures is essential for both individuals navigating their careers and organizations designing competitive pay strategies.

What trends make this a crucial area of study in 2024?

Beyond macroeconomic pressures, the explosive growth of artificial intelligence has introduced a new and urgent dimension to compensation research. The demand for AI-related talent has surged dramatically in recent years, creating a structural shift in how employers compete for skilled workers. Research supports the scale of this transformation: Analysis of nearly 15,000 data science salary records from 2020 to 2024 found a consistent upward trend in compensation with 2023 marking the sharpest single-year increase (Bagyam (2024)), and AI-specialized roles commanding the highest salaries of all job categories. This rapid reshaping of salary structures, driven by AI adoption and talent shortages, makes 2024 a particularly critical moment to study compensation trends. Our project is motivated by the need to capture and understand these shifts as they unfold, providing timely insights into a labor market that is transforming faster than ever before.

What do you expect to find in your analysis?

Based on the analysis of 2021 to 2023 data science compensation (Saeeji, Hayder, and Younis (2025)), we would expect a 2024 analysis to show continued overall salary growth, with the highest pay concentrated among experienced professionals, full-time employees, contractors in some cases, and senior technical roles, while salary outcomes still vary substantially by company size and location. The article also suggests that higher-paying opportunities are concentrated in select U.S. markets, with Illinois and the United States standing out in its sample, and that pay remains uneven overall, with most workers clustered in lower-to-mid salary bands and a smaller group earning very high salaries. This expectation of an uneven wage distribution is further supported by research on AI and wage inequality (Georgieff (2024)) which highlights how the rapid integration of artificial intelligence tends to widen the salary gap between high-skilled tech roles and traditional occupations. Therefore, in our analysis of the Lightcast dataset, we expect to find a distinct “AI wage premium,” where roles requiring AI competencies significantly outperform non-AI careers in average compensation. Regarding industry-specific impacts, we expect this premium to boost tech, finance, and professional services’ wages the most in 2024. These sectors rapidly adopted AI, so growth will be sharper there. Manufacturing and retail might see slower wage gains. Looking at the analysis of approximately ten million online job vacancies from 2018 to 2024 (Castaneda, Bone, and Stephany (2026)), we also expect AI-related careers in 2024 to outperform comparable non-AI roles not only in pay but also in job quality, since AI postings were associated with higher salaries and were significantly more likely to include benefits such as remote work, parental leave, and workplace-culture perks. This article also unveils trends in remote-work by showing that remote options are more common in AI postings than non-AI postings, however, it does not directly establish whether remote jobs pay more than in-office roles overall. By analyzing the Lightcast dataset, we aim to bridge this gap and clarify the intersection of remote work, location, and salary. Both articles and the OECD findings mainly support stronger pay and richer benefits for AI talent and continued location-based pay differences in high-paying U.S. markets which we look forward to validating in the Lightcast jobs dataset.
