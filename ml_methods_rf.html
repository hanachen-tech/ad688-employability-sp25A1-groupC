Model Performance

The Random Forest model achieved an R² of 0.526 and Adjusted R² of 0.517, explaining approximately 52% of salary variation. Compared to the Multiple Linear Regression model (R² = 0.378), Random Forest improved explanatory power by nearly 15 percentage points. The lower RMSE ($30,791) and MAE ($22,192) also confirm better prediction accuracy. The minimal gap between R² and Adjusted R² indicates that the features included in the model contribute meaningfully rather than inflating performance artificially.

Feature Importance

Unlike MLR which outputs coefficients, Random Forest provides feature importance scores that indicate how much each variable contributes to salary prediction.

The most influential variable is employment type (Full-time > 32 hours), with an importance score of 0.360 — far exceeding all other features. This suggests that whether a job is full-time or part-time has the strongest impact on salary, more so than job title or skills.

Among job titles, Data Engineer Analytics stands out as the highest-paying role (importance: 0.059), consistent with strong market demand for data engineering skills. Among skills, communication (0.085) and planning (0.076) rank highest, suggesting that soft skills play a meaningful role in salary differentiation alongside technical abilities.

Geographic location (state-level) shows relatively low importance across all states, indicating that location alone is not a strong salary predictor once job title, skills, and employment type are controlled for.

Model Limitations

Despite the improvement over MLR, the model still leaves approximately 48% of salary variance unexplained. Key factors not available in the dataset — such as years of experience, education level, company size, and individual negotiation — likely account for the remaining variance. Future work could incorporate these variables to further improve predictive accuracy.
