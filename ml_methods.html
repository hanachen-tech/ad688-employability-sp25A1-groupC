Model Performance

To model salary based on location, job title, and skills, we applied Multiple Linear Regression using PySpark’s ML pipeline on 30,808 job postings with valid salary data. The model used the top 20 job titles, all states, and three core skill indicators (Python, SQL, Excel) as predictors.

The model achieved an R² of 0.269, explaining approximately 27% of salary variation. The RMSE of $38,254 and MAE of $29,050 indicate that prediction errors remain substantial, suggesting that location, job title, and a limited skill set alone are insufficient to fully explain salary differences.

Salary Impact by Feature

The coefficient table shows the direction and magnitude of each feature’s relationship with salary, relative to the model’s baseline.

Job Title shows the clearest pattern: Data Analyst II, Business Intelligence Analyst, and Retail Data Analyst roles are all associated with notably lower salaries, with coefficients ranging from -$61,618 to -$75,429 relative to the baseline. This suggests entry-level and specialized analyst roles tend to pay less than the reference category.

Geographic location shows less differentiation. States such as Montana, Ohio, Tennessee, Florida, and Georgia all cluster closely together with similarly high coefficients (~$182,000–185,000), indicating that the model does not strongly distinguish salary differences across states once other factors are accounted for. The intercept of -$19,603, while not directly interpretable as a real salary value, reflects the mathematical baseline from which all coefficients are measured — actual predicted salaries are derived by combining the intercept with the relevant state, title, and skill coefficients.

Model Limitations

With only 27% of variance explained, this baseline model indicates that job title, state, and basic technical skills (Python, SQL, Excel) capture only part of what drives salary outcomes. Important factors such as years of experience, education level, company size, and soft skills are not included in this version, which likely explains the relatively high prediction error. Expanding the feature set — for example, incorporating employment type, remote work status, and a broader range of skills — could improve model performance, as demonstrated in subsequent iterations of this analysis.
